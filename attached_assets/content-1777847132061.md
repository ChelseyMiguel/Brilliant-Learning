# Learning Objectives

When we are born, we have our own unique genetic code and biological features. However, our identity as human beings develops through our interactions with others in society. Many experts in both psychology and sociology have described the process of self-development as a crucial step to understanding how that "self" becomes part of society.

## Psychological Perspectives on Self-Development

Sigmund
FreudSigmund Freud (1856–1939), a psychoanalyst, was one of the
most important modern scientists to propose a theory about how people develop
their sense of self. He divided the process of growing up into stages and
suggested that a person's self-development is closely tied to these early stages
of life.

According to Freud, if someone struggles to properly engage with or move past a
particular stage, it can lead to emotional and psychological problems later in
adulthood.

![Open assessment](https://learnyourway.withgoogle.com/static/immersive-text/spark_info_2.svg)

Erik
EriksonPsychologist Erik Erikson (1902–1994) developed a theory of
personality that was partly based on Freud's work. However, Erikson believed
that personality continues to change throughout life and is never truly
finished. His theory includes eight stages of development, starting at birth and
ending at death. Erikson proposed that people move through these stages over
their entire lives. Unlike Freud, who focused on psychosexual stages and basic
human desires, Erikson's view of self-development emphasized social factors. He
looked at how we balance our own basic desires with what society accepts
(Erikson 1982).

![Open assessment](https://learnyourway.withgoogle.com/static/immersive-text/spark_info_2.svg)

Jean
PiagetJean Piaget (1896–1980) was a psychologist who focused on
how social interactions play a role in child development. He observed that the
development of one's self happens as a person balances their internal
understanding of the world with their actual experiences of the world through
social interactions (Piaget 1954). All three of these thinkers have
helped us understand self-development in modern times.

![Open assessment](https://learnyourway.withgoogle.com/static/immersive-text/spark_info_2.svg)

Harry
HarlowPsychologist Harry Harlow (1905–1981) is well-known for
studying the importance of social relationships in human development. He did
this by observing how isolation and a lack of maternal care affected the
development of young rhesus monkeys. Harlow discovered that monkeys who were
isolated showed disturbed behaviors, including self-harm, and had trouble
fitting in when they were later reunited with other monkeys. Harlow also gave
isolated monkeys fake "mothers"—some were covered in cloth, while others were
made of wire. Based on their behavior, the young monkeys seemed to form
attachments to the cloth mothers and relied on them for emotional comfort. Even
more than seventy years later, this experiment continues to influence research
by psychologists, sociologists, and anyone interested in human
development.

> ### SOCIOLOGICAL RESEARCH
>
> #### Sociology or Psychology: What’s the Difference?
>
> You might wonder: if both sociologists and psychologists are interested in people and their behavior, how are these two academic fields different? What do they agree on, and where do their ideas differ? The answers are complex, but the distinction is important to scholars in both fields.
>
> Generally, we can say that while both fields study human behavior, psychologists focus on how the mind influences that behavior, whereas sociologists examine how society shapes behavior. Psychologists are interested in people's mental growth and how their minds interpret the world around them. Sociologists are more likely to focus on how various aspects of society contribute to an individual's connection with their world. Another way to think about the difference is that psychologists often look inward (at mental health, emotional processes), while sociologists tend to look outward (at social institutions, cultural norms, interactions with others) to understand human behavior.

Émile
DurkheimÉmile Durkheim (1858–1917) was the first to make this
distinction in his research. He explained differences in suicide rates among
people by pointing to social reasons (like religious differences) rather than
psychological reasons (like an individual's mental well-being) (Durkheim 1897).
Today, we still see this same distinction.
For example, a sociologist studying why
a reader chooses a specific book might focus on social trends in literary
genres, the influence of book clubs and online reviews, or how reading habits
differ across socioeconomic groups. A psychologist would more likely be
interested in the reader's personal memories associated with certain stories or
the cognitive processes involved in interpreting the text.

> Sometimes sociologists and psychologists have worked together to expand knowledge. In recent decades, however, their fields have become more clearly separate. Sociologists increasingly focus on large societal issues and patterns, while psychologists remain concentrated on the human mind. Both fields make valuable contributions through different approaches, providing us with distinct types of useful insights.
